Qubit modalities

A working comparison of the major physical platforms being used to build qubits today — where each one is strong, where it struggles, and roughly how the numbers stack up. Figures below are typical ranges from current lab and early commercial systems, not records; any given group can beat them.

In detail

Superconducting circuits (transmon)

Google, IBM, Rigetti, IQM

Qubits are anharmonic LC oscillators made from Josephson junctions, controlled with microwave pulses and read out through a dispersively coupled cavity — the architecture behind most of the field's largest processors.

Pros

  • Fast gates (tens of ns) and fast, high-fidelity readout
  • Lithographically fabricated — leverages existing chip-making infrastructure
  • Flexible coupling topology, tunable frequencies
  • Most mature control electronics and software stack

Cons

  • Short coherence relative to atomic qubits (dielectric and quasiparticle loss)
  • Needs dilution refrigeration (~10 mK)
  • Wiring density becomes a bottleneck as qubit count grows
  • Sensitive to two-level-system defects and stray TLS in materials

Trapped ion

IonQ, Quantinuum, Alpine Quantum Technologies

Individual ions confined in electromagnetic traps encode qubits in long-lived electronic or hyperfine states, manipulated with laser or microwave pulses; entanglement is mediated through shared motional modes.

Pros

  • Longest coherence times of any mainstream platform
  • Highest reported two-qubit gate fidelities
  • All-to-all connectivity within a trap
  • Every ion of a given species is identical — no fabrication variance

Cons

  • Gate speeds are slow compared to solid-state qubits
  • Scaling requires ion shuttling or photonic interconnects between traps
  • Complex laser and vacuum-system engineering
  • Crosstalk grows as more ions share a trap's motional modes

Neutral atom (Rydberg)

QuEra, Pasqal, Atom Computing

Individual neutral atoms are held in optical tweezer arrays and excited to Rydberg states to mediate strong, controllable interactions for entangling gates.

Pros

  • Arrays are reconfigurable in real time via optical tweezers
  • Excellent qubit-count scaling — hundreds to thousands demonstrated
  • Long coherence in the ground-state encoding
  • Natural fit for analog quantum simulation as well as gate-based computing

Cons

  • Rydberg-state gates are comparatively slow and sensitive to laser noise
  • Atom loss requires re-loading / error mitigation strategies
  • Mid-circuit readout and atom movement add latency
  • Younger error-correction track record than superconducting or ion platforms

Spin qubits (semiconductor quantum dots)

Intel, Diraq, Silicon Quantum Computing

A single electron or hole spin confined in a gate-defined quantum dot, typically in isotopically purified silicon, encodes the qubit; control is via microwave or electric-dipole spin resonance.

Pros

  • Extremely small footprint — potentially the densest qubit packing
  • Compatible with existing CMOS foundry processes
  • Long coherence achievable in isotopically purified ²⁸Si
  • Fast gates with room for on-chip classical control integration

Cons

  • Device-to-device variability from fabrication disorder
  • Still needs cryogenic operation, though less extreme than transmons
  • Two-qubit coupling is short-range, so architecture is constrained
  • Fewer qubits demonstrated at scale so far than superconducting or ion systems

NV / color centers (diamond)

Quantum Brilliance, academic groups

A point defect in diamond (nitrogen-vacancy or related color centers) hosts an electron spin qubit that can be optically initialized and read out, with nearby nuclear spins as long-lived memory qubits.

Pros

  • Can operate at room temperature
  • Optical interface makes it naturally suited to quantum networking / sensing
  • Nearby nuclear spins offer very long-lived quantum memory
  • Mature as a quantum sensor, derisking parts of the technology

Cons

  • Defects are randomly positioned — hard to place deterministically at scale
  • Optical readout and coupling efficiency are relatively low
  • Two-qubit gates between distant centers are slow (photon-mediated)
  • Far fewer qubits demonstrated than the leading compute-focused platforms

Photonic

PsiQuantum, Xanadu

Qubits are encoded in properties of single photons (polarization, path, or continuous-variable states) and manipulated with linear optics, sometimes combined with measurement-based entangling operations.

Pros

  • Photons don't decohere from their environment in flight — ideal for networking
  • Naturally interfaces with existing fiber-optic infrastructure
  • Can operate at or near room temperature (excluding single-photon detectors)
  • Strong native fit for distributed / modular quantum computing

Cons

  • Entangling gates are inherently probabilistic without exotic resources
  • Photon loss directly translates to computational error
  • Requires large numbers of single-photon sources and detectors
  • No natural "memory" — photons must be used or lost

Topological (Majorana-based)

Microsoft

A more speculative approach that aims to encode quantum information non-locally in the topology of exotic quasiparticles (such as Majorana zero modes), so that local noise cannot easily cause errors.

Pros

  • Error protection is built into the physics, not bolted on afterward
  • If realized, could need far less error-correction overhead than other platforms
  • Potentially compatible with semiconductor-nanowire fabrication

Cons

  • Unambiguous experimental demonstration of the qubit itself remains contested
  • No working multi-qubit processor has been shown yet
  • Materials science (clean topological superconductor-semiconductor interfaces) is extremely demanding
  • Highest scientific risk of any platform on this list
